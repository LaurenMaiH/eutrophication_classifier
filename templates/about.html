What is Lake Eutrophication?

Lake eutrophication is a process that occurs when a surplus of a nutrient is introduced to a lake, leading to a subsequent algal bloom. This bloom causes the water's oxygen levels to fall drastically and lowers the pH of the water, leading to the dying off of marine life. Nutrient influxes capable of causing eutrophication are increasingly caused by human activity. Fertilizer runoff, sewage, and industrial waste products, among other human byproducts, pollute bodies of water and flood the ecosystem with an overabundance of nutrients. Eutrophication is a serious environmental concern; the habitats it affects could die and be unable to support life.

How Does the Eutrophication Classifier Address This Problem?

My project is designed to help address this problem by identifying how threatened a given lake is by analyzing an image of its water. I trained a multiclassification model to be able to identify a picture of water as hypereutrophic, eutrophic, mesotrophic, or oligotrophic (oligotrophic being good water quality). The assessment can then be used to take action accordingly. For example, if a lake is classified as mesotrophic, we could improve phosphate-stripping processes at sewage plants nearby or reduce fertilizer input.










Sinha Mahira Sultana's Lake Image Classification dataset from Kaggle was used to train this model.
